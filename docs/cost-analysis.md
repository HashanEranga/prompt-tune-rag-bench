## How these numbers are derived

Nothing here is guessed. The estimator reads the frozen dataset and counts real tokens with
`tiktoken`:

| Measured input | Value |
|---|---|
| Test questions | 100 (mean **14 tokens** — they're short) |
| System prompt | **42 tokens**, identical for every producer |
| Mean retrievable section | **87 tokens** → RAG context = 3 × 87 = **261 tokens** |
| `train.jsonl` | **25,853 tokens** × 3 epochs = **77,559 trained tokens** |
| Output charged at | **300 tokens/answer** — the cap, i.e. the worst case |

The one deliberate pessimism: **every answer is billed the full 300-token output cap**, even
though a typical answer is ~60 tokens. That makes the total a **ceiling**. Real spend should
land meaningfully below it — plausibly around **$2–3 for Phase B** once answers come in at
their natural length.

## Where the money actually goes

### 1. Output dominates input, by roughly 5×

Per producer: **5,678 input tokens** vs **30,000 output tokens**. The questions are tiny and
the system prompt is 42 tokens, so there is almost nothing to *send* — the cost is in what
comes back. Consequence: **`MAX_OUTPUT_TOKENS` is the single most powerful cost dial in the
project.** Halving it roughly halves inference spend.

### 2. Fine-tuning now costs $0.00 — and the reason is not frugality

The projection below was the plan. It never ran:

| Rung *(projected, never executed)* | Training | Inference | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ft-openai-small` (nano) | $0.12 | $0.03 | $0.15 |
| `ft-openai-medium` (mini) | $0.39 | $0.10 | $0.49 |
| **`ft-openai-large` (gpt-4.1)** | **$1.94** | **$0.50** | **$2.44** |

This analysis concluded *"$2.44 is cheap for the finding — keep it."* That recommendation was
overtaken by events: the platform was withdrawn (see **RESOLVED** below) and the money could
not be spent at any price. What actually shipped:

| Rung *(executed)* | Base | Train time | Peak VRAM | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ft-local-small` | `Qwen2.5-0.5B` | 2.3 min | 1.69 GB | **$0.00** |
| `ft-local-medium` | `Qwen2.5-1.5B` | 3.7 min | 3.54 GB | **$0.00** |
| `ft-local-large` | `Qwen2.5-3B` | 7.2 min | 5.24 GB | **$0.00** |
| `ft-llama3.1-8b` | `Llama-3.1-8B` | *on Together* | *~15 GB — won't fit* | **$4.00** |

**13.2 minutes of GPU time, $0.00, and the full size ladder the brief asks for** — trained
inside 8 GB with 2.8 GB to spare at the top rung. The honest unit here is GPU-hours (0.22),
not dollars.

### 2b. The one thing worth paying for — and the 100× markup we paid for it

`ft-llama3.1-8b` is the only producer that costs money to *make*, and the only one the hardware
**cannot** make: an 8B needs ~15 GB to fine-tune (our peaks scale 1.69 → 3.54 → 5.24 GB at 0.5
→ 1.5 → 3B) but only ~5.5 GB to *serve* at 4-bit — and this box already runs `llama3.1:8b` at
6.3 GB. So Together is used as a **rented trainer, not a host**: train there, **download the
adapter**, serve here forever for $0. No endpoint, no hourly meter.

**But look closely at what $4.00 actually bought:**

| | |
|---|---|
| Together's LoRA rate (≤16B) | $0.48 / 1M training tokens |
| Our trained tokens (300 pairs × 3 epochs) | **77,559** |
| Tokens actually consumed, at their own rate | **$0.04** |
| **Charged** | **$4.00** — *"Each job is subject to a minimum charge of $4.00"* |
| **Effective markup** | **~100×** |

We did not pay for compute. We paid a **per-job floor**, and at this dataset size the floor *is*
the price — the tokens are rounding error. That is a real property of managed fine-tuning that a
per-token price table completely hides: **small jobs are priced as if they were large ones.**
Anyone fine-tuning on a few hundred examples is buying a minimum, not a rate.

It was still the right $4. It bought a capability we did not have, and an artefact we keep.
Re-training a *Qwen* rung on Together — same base, same data, same method as `ft-local-medium` —
would have paid the identical $4 to reproduce something the laptop already did free in 3.7
minutes. **The floor is only worth paying when you're buying something you can't self-host.**

### 3. The judge costs more than everything it judges — by 17×

**Phase C ($6.87 batched ceiling) > Phase B inference ($0.3946 actual), by ~17×.** This surprises
people, and the reason is structural and worth stating plainly:

> **A producer is billed for the question it reads. The judge is billed for the whole document.**

Phase B's producers each read a **14-token question**. The judge reads a **969-token source
document** — plus the rubric, plus the answer — and it does that **1,000 times**. Mean judge input
is **2,022 tokens per call**, ~144× the question a producer sees. The input side, which was
rounding error all through Phase B, becomes the dominant term the moment you start judging.

That is also why the judge's prompt design *is* a cost decision. Feeding it the full document
rather than the tagged 100-token section is what pushes the input from ~380 to ~2,022 tokens/call
— roughly **4× the Phase C bill**. We paid it deliberately: the section-only prompt would mark an
answer unfaithful for correctly citing a *neighbouring* section, i.e. punish the model for our own
segmentation. **$4 to not corrupt the measurement is the cheapest thing in this project.**

**The judge does not care that six of your ten producers are free.** It pays the same per answer
whether that answer came from `gpt-4.1` or from a free local adapter. Adding `ft-llama3.1-8b`
cost **$0.00** in inference — and **~$0.69** at the judge. Scaling the roster is nearly free on the
producer side and strictly linear on the judge side, and the judge side is the expensive one.

**So the real price of a producer is its judge bill, not its inference bill.** That is the number
to reason with when deciding whether a contender earns its place. `ft-llama3.1-8b` earns it: for
~$4.69 all-in it closes the TRIAD, which is the one comparison the whole project is named after.

**Two knobs halve or double this, and both are deliberate:**

| Knob | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Batches API** (on) | **−50% on every token.** Scoring is the least latency-sensitive job in the project; nothing waits on the verdicts. The synchronous premium would buy nothing at all. |
| **Adaptive thinking** (off) | On would take the ceiling **$6.87 → $9.87**. The rubric is a bounded 5-field judgement against a document already in the prompt — not a puzzle. `--thinking` is there if you want to pay for a more deliberative judge. |

### 4. Half the project is free

Six producers cost exactly **$0**: both Ollama prompting models, all three local QLoRA rungs, and
the `llama3.1:8b` RAG pipeline (including all embeddings). That isn't just a budget convenience —
it's *itself a finding*. If a free, self-hosted 8B model with RAG scores competitively against a
paid frontier model, that's the headline of the report, and the cost column is what proves it.

## What is NOT counted

- **Electricity / GPU wear** on the local QLoRA runs. Reported as **GPU-hours** in
  `results/finetune_jobs.json` instead of dollars — the honest unit for self-hosted work.
- **Your time.** In practice the real cost of the local backend is debugging, not compute.
- **Retries.** Transient failures are retried up to 4×; a pathological rate-limit storm could
  add a little. Immaterial at this scale.
- **Re-runs.** Effectively zero: `answers.jsonl` is keyed on `(question_id, producer)` and every
  command resumes, so a crash or Ctrl-C never pays for the same call twice.

## RESOLVED — OpenAI fine-tuning is gone

This was filed as an open risk on **2026-07-13**. It landed on **2026-07-14**. Submitting the
first training job returned:

```
openai.PermissionDeniedError: 403 - training_not_available
  "OpenAI is winding down the fine-tuning platform and your organization is
   no longer able to create new fine-tuning training jobs."
```

The revocation is at the **organisation** level, not per-model: no base model, no retry and no
key gets around it. The three `ft-openai-*` producers were removed from `models.py`, and
`finetune --backend openai` now exits with that 403 rather than silently selecting zero
producers.

**What it cost, and what it didn't:**

| | budgeted | after OpenAI died | **shipped** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Producers | 12 | 9 | **10** |
| Answers | 1,200 | 900 | **1,000** |
| Phase B — FT training | $2.44 | $0.00 | **$4.00** *(Together, once)* |
| Phase B — inference | $1.26 | $0.83 | **$0.83** |
| Phase C — judge | $5.05 | $3.78 | **$4.21** |
| **Project total** | **$8.75** | **$4.62** | **$9.04** |

### Where managed fine-tuning actually went

Every provider was checked. **Gemini** dropped tuning from the API entirely (Vertex only, behind
GCP service-account auth). **Groq**'s is closed beta. **OpenRouter** is a router with no training
stack. **Together** and **Fireworks** still offer it. Sort that list by who owns the weights and
the pattern is unmistakable:

> **Every provider still offering fine-tuning tunes OPEN-WEIGHT models.
> Every provider withdrawing it owns closed frontier weights.**

The reasons are structural: a fine-tuning endpoint is a capability-extraction surface (it lets
customers distill frontier behaviour into weights they then own); bespoke checkpoints wreck
serving economics; reasoning models don't take supervised fine-tuning well (which is why the one
surviving OpenAI option is *reinforcement* fine-tuning at **$100/hour**); and the labs' own pitch
has moved to *"don't fine-tune — use context and retrieval."*

**That last point should stop you.** It is *this project's thesis*. Our Step 2 result — a fluent,
confidently invented *"$500 to $1,000 per day"* against a truth of 1,000 LKR — reproduces, at
0.5B on a laptop GPU, the reasoning behind an industry-wide product retreat. **Fine-tuning has
become an open-weights capability, not a frontier-model one.**

**This is the finding, and it belongs in the final report.** The headline cost conclusion was
going to be *"managed fine-tuning costs $2.44; self-hosting costs electricity."* The real one is
stronger and less comfortable, and it has three parts:

1. **You could not buy the managed option at any price** — the vendor withdrew it mid-project.
2. **Where you still can buy it, small jobs are priced as minimums, not rates** — $4.00 for
   $0.04 of tokens, a ~100× markup.
3. **The self-hosted path had a property no price table captures: it still existed on the day we
   needed it.** And it cost nothing to discover, because the ladder was built both ways.

The $4 we *did* spend bought the one thing self-hosting couldn't give us — an 8B fine-tune the
card cannot train — and we downloaded the adapter so we own it. **Rent the capability you lack;
never rent the one you already have.**

**The estimator was right, and right in the safe direction.** Inference landed at 48% of its
ceiling, exactly as predicted, because answers averaged ~123 tokens against the 300-token cap
charged. No `PRICING` row proved wrong. The one line that did *not* come in under was the
Together training floor — because a floor is a price, not a projection.

**The lesson transfers to Phase C.** The judge estimate ($6.87 batched) charges every verdict the
full 512-token output cap; real verdicts run ~120 tokens. Expect the same 2–3× overshoot, landing
around **$2.80–$4.20** — and if it lands *above* $6.87, the `PRICING` table is wrong and should be
fixed before anything else is believed.

Better to learn that at $3 than at $50 — which is the entire reason this step exists.
